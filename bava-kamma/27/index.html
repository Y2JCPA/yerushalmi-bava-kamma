📖 Overview — When Liability Transfers (and When It Doesn't)

נָפְלָה לַגִּינָּה וְהִזִּיקָה מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶׁנֶּהֱנֵית. יָרְדָה כְדַרְכָּהּ וְהִזִּיקָה מְשַׁלֶּמֶת מַה שֶׁהִזִּיקָה
The sugya: The daf finishes the corral Mishnah by asking what counts as 'walled,' brings R' Yehoshua's striking list of four cases that are exempt in court but not in heaven, and then turns to Mishnah 6:2 — the rich case-tree of an animal grazing in someone else's field. The big distinction: did it fall in (paying only what it benefited), or did it enter normally (paying the actual damage)? R' Yochanan + Reish Lakish raise the bar: until the animal exits to the public domain and re-enters another field, the owner stays exempt.

🧱 Three-Walled Corrals and Fallen Walls

When does inadequate fencing equal liability?
1️⃣ Mishnah was about a fully-walled corral (4 sides).
↓
2️⃣ Question: what if 3 sides are walled and one side is breached?
↓
3️⃣ Beraita answer: a wall fell from earthquakes or thunder. If he rebuilt it properly → פָּטוּר. If not → חַיָיב.
↓
4️⃣ The proof works because rebuilding restores normal shemira; failure to rebuild is the same as choosing to leave the breach.
↓
5️⃣ Caveat: only an issue when the fallen wall comes down on its own — not when the other three walls were brittle and collapsed too.
Practical principle: Shemira liability is dynamic. Force majeure starts the clock; a reasonable rebuild stops it. Negligence after the event re-engages liability.

⚖️ R' Yehoshua's Four Cases — Exempt but Owed

אֵין חַיָיבִין מִן הַדִּין וְאֵין הַשָּׁמַיִם מוֹחֲלִין
PersonActionWhy exempt בדין
הַיּוֹדֵעַ עֵדוּתKnows testimony, refuses to testifyNegative omission, not an act of damage
הַשּׂוֹכֵר עֵידֵי שֶׁקֶרHires false witnesses and collectsThe 'damage' was done by the witnesses' false testimony
הַכּוֹבֵשׁ קָמָה לִפְנֵי הָאוֹרBends standing grain toward a fireIndirect — gerama — not legally cognizable
הַפּוֹרֵץ גָּדֵר לִפְנֵי בְהֵמָהBreaches a fence so an animal can escape and damageIndirect — opened the path, didn't cause directly
The split: Beit Din uses standards of direct causation. Heaven measures intent. R' Yehoshua makes both visible — the law releases you, but the moral debt stays.

🔓 Robbers Who Released the Flock

Why they took it changes who pays.
M
הוֹצִיאוּהָ לִיסְטִין הַלִּיסְטִין חַיָיבִין — robbers who let it out are liable.
Rav H
Only if they let it out לְגוֹזְלָהּ — to steal it. Then they took ownership/responsibility.
Rav H
But if they let it out לְאַבְּדָהּ — just to lose it — the robbers are פְּטוּרִין.
Why intent matters: Theft creates a kinyan-like relationship: the robbers become the animal's effective owners and inherit liability. Pure malicious release leaves no such tie — and the original owner's shemira lapsed first.

🌱 Falling vs. Entering — Two Tort Standards

The Mishnah 6:2 distinction.

🪂 נָפְלָה לְגִנָּה

  • Fell in by accident
  • Pays only מַה שֶׁנֶּהֱנֵית
  • Rav Huna: works only if it landed on grass; if it stood and grazed → full damage

🚶 יָרְדָה כְדַרְכָּהּ

  • Walked in normally
  • Pays מַה שֶׁהִזִּיקָה — actual damage
  • Estimate by beit-se'ah method

🌽 R' Shimon's Refinement

  • Ate ripe produce → pays in produce
  • אִם סְאָה סְאָה אִם סָאתַיִם סָאתַיִם
  • 1-for-1 by quantity
Why the gap: An animal falling in didn't 'choose' to damage; the owner didn't release it. So liability is reduced to the value the animal received — restitution, not full tort. Walking in normally re-engages full liability.

🌾 R' Yochanan / Reish Lakish — How Far Does Exemption Go?

When 'in due course' includes leaving and re-entering.
Animal grazes through a field
↓
R' Yose ben Chanina: only that field is exempt. A second field → liable.
↓
R' Yochanan / RL
Even grazing the entire field is exempt — until it leaves to רשות הרבים and enters a new field
Edge case
Fell into one garden, walked into another? Disagreement
↓
Both Mishnah clauses are needed — one teaches מה שנהנית (fall), the other מה שהזיקה (entrance)
The geography of liability: An animal isn't fully a maazik until its trespass crosses into the public domain. Internal grazing remains under the falling-in regime.
