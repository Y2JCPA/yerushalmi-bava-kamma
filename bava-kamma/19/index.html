📖 Overview — Selective Notoriety and Whose Ox Is Liable

שׁוֹר שֶׁהוּא מוּעָד לְמִינוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מוּעָד לְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ... אֵימָתַי הוּא תָּם מִשֶּׁיַּחֲזוֹר בּוֹ שְׁלֹשֶׁת יְמֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת
The sugya: Daf 19 covers a rich stretch. It opens with a partnership-loss puzzle — when investment items are stolen, do all partners share the loss equally or proportionally? It then moves to the Mishnah of selective mu'ad: an ox notorious for goring its own kind but not other species, humans but not animals, small ones but not large ones, weekdays vs. Shabbat. The Yerushalmi links this to a parallel niddah debate about non-continuous bleeding. Finally the daf turns to oxen of hekdesh and of gentiles, ending with the dramatic story of the two Roman officials sent to study Torah with Rabban Gamliel.

🪙 Stolen Coins vs. Stolen Stones

How a partnership absorbs theft depends on what was stolen.

🟡 סְלָעִים גַּסּוֹת (large coins)

  • Identifiable, divisible units
  • אֶיפְשַׁר לִבְלוֹל לָצֵאת יְדֵי כוּלָּן — possible to subdivide exactly
  • Loss can be split cleanly proportional to investment

💎 אֲבָנִים דַּקּוֹת (small stones)

  • Indistinguishable individual units
  • לֵית יְדַע אִי מִן הָדֵין נְסַב אִי מִן הָדֵין נְסַב
  • Doubt → each takes by his purse

⚖️ R' Yose b. R' Bun's worry

  • If 50 stolen out of 50+150…
  • Equal-loss rule lets the small investor lose nothing
  • נִמְצֵאת מִידַּת הַדִּין לוֹקָה — equity is hurt
The technical move: R' Shammai distinguishes objects that can be re-tallied (coins) from objects that can't (small stones). Where re-tallying fails, the doubt itself reshapes the rule.

🐂 Selective Mu'ad — The Mishnah's Categories

An ox can be notorious for one target and tame for another.
שור שהוא מועד למינו ואינו מועד לשאינו מינו
↓
Damage to the target it is מועד for → נזק שלם
Damage to a different target → חצי נזק
↓
By species
מועד לְמינו vs. שאינו מינו
By victim type
מועד לאדם vs. בהמה
By size
מועד לקטנים vs. גדולים
By day (R' Yehudah)
מועד בשבתות vs. ימות החול
↓
Reverts to תם only after שְׁלֹשֶׁת יְמֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת of changed behavior
Yerushalmi's question: Why would Shabbat make a difference? מִכֵּיוָן דּוּ חֲמִי לוֹן לְבִישִׁין נְקִיִין עוֹד הוּא מְשַׁנֶּה דַּעְתֵּיהּ — when the ox sees people in clean clothes, it changes its behavior.

🩸 The Niddah Parallel — Rav Ada vs. Rav Huna

Can three goring incidents on three different days make one mu'ad?
Q
What if the ox gored on day 1, was kept in on day 2, and gored on day 3? Does the unattested middle day break the chazakah?
RA
Rav Ada b. Achwa quoting Rav: parallel to the niddah who checked, found tamei, skipped a day, then found tamei again — נִדָּה וַדַּאי. Definitely a niddah.
RH
Rav Huna quoting Rav: נִדָּה סָפֵק. Doubtful. The gap matters.
RH+
Rav Huna explains: I was there at the beginning, middle, and end of the conversation. Rav first said certain, reversed himself, ended with safek. Rav Ada only heard the first answer.
Why the parallel works: Both cases ask the same structural question: does an unattested middle day break a presumptive chazakah? The Yerushalmi imports the niddah debate to illuminate the goring case.

🛕 Whose Ox Triggers Liability

The Mishnah's third halakhah maps liability across owner categories.
Goring ox belongs to…Damaged ox belongs to…Liability
IsraelHekdeshExempt — שׁוֹר רֵעֵהוּ, not Temple
HekdeshIsraelExempt — same derashah
IsraelNon-JewExempt
Non-JewIsraelFull damages — tam or mu'ad
Non-JewNon-Jew (judged by Israel's law)Full damages — כְּדִינֵיהֶן
The aggadot behind the law: Rav: רָאָה וַיַּתֵּר גּוֹיִם — He saw and 'permitted' the gentiles, i.e., released their property to those they oppressed. R' Yose b. Chaninah: He impoverished them. R' Yochanan (via R' Abbahu): כְּדִינֵיהֶן — by their own laws.

👑 The Two Roman Officials and Rabban Gamliel

A daf-ending story with serious halakhic teeth.
1️⃣ The Roman government sent two איסרטיוטות (officials) to study Torah with Rabban Gamliel.
↓
2️⃣ They learned Mikra, Mishnah, Talmud, halakhot, and aggadot.
↓
3️⃣ At the end they objected to three rulings: midwifery, nursing, and stolen property of a non-Jew.
↓
4️⃣ Specifically the rule that שׁוֹר שֶׁלְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁנָּגַח לְשׁוֹר שֶׁלַּנָּכְרִי פָּטוּר.
↓
5️⃣ Rabban Gamliel decreed that gezel akum is forbidden מִפְּנֵי חִילּוּל הָשֵׁם — to prevent desecration of God's Name. Yet by the time the officials reached the Tyrian ladder, they had forgotten everything.
The story's punch: Halakhah is reshaped under the gaze of outsiders. Rabban Gamliel does not deny what is taught; he tightens the practical rule so the Torah will not be desecrated. The miraculous forgetting then preserves the original Mishnaic letter.
