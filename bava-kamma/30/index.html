📖 Overview — From Trustees to Thieves

מְרוּבָּה מִידַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל מִמִּידַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה
The sugya: The daf opens with R' Yochanan's question — can a beit din advance an argument the party did not make? — illustrated by the strange Bar Ziza estate case. It then turns to the Mishnah of fire damage (spark, camel, storekeeper) and R' Yehudah's Hanukkah-light exemption. The page closes by opening a new chapter on theft: כפל covers everything alive or inanimate, while ארבעה וחמשה apply only to ox and sheep.

🪙 The Bar Ziza Case

A sharecropper's deposit becomes a courtroom puzzle.
1️⃣ Bar Ziza's sharecropper deposits a pound of gold (לִיטְרָא דְהַב) with a third party.
↓
2️⃣ Both Bar Ziza and the sharecropper die.
↓
3️⃣ R' Yishmael b. R' Yose: everyone knows a sharecropper has nothing of his own — give it to Bar Ziza's sons; older sons take half now, younger half when they grow up.
↓
4️⃣ R' Chiyya overrules: that argument לֵית שְׁמַע מִינָּהּ כְּלוּם — give it instead to the sharecropper's sons.
↓
5️⃣ R' Yitzchak's framing: between older and younger sons it is כְּמִי שֶׁנִּיתַּן לָהֶן מַתָּנָה — treated as a gift, not a claim.
Why this story is here: R' Yochanan asked whether a court can volunteer arguments the litigants themselves did not raise. The Bar Ziza incident is the test case — and it shows that what a beit din ruled stands, even after the next court rules differently.

🔥 Mishnah: Three Cases of Fire

Each scenario shifts liability based on whose action triggered the spread.

🔨 הפטיש

  • גֵּץ שֶׁיָּצָא מִתַּחַת הַפַּטִּישׁ
  • Spark flies from a hammer and damages
  • Worker is liable — his act caused the spark

🐪 הגמל

  • Camel loaded with flax in רשות הרבים
  • Flax enters a shop and ignites on the lamp
  • Camel owner is liable — he overloaded into the shop

🪔 החנווני

  • If the storekeeper put his lamp outside
  • He is the one liable
  • R' Yehudah: בְּנֵר חֲנוּכָּה פָטוּר
Pivot point: The Mishnah moves liability case by case based on whose normal-use act exposed the property to fire. R' Yehudah's exemption for a Hanukkah light raises the harder question: can a mitzvah excuse you from damage liability?

💬 Hanukkah Light: Permission or Liability?

R' Yehudah and the Sages disagree on whether רשות frees you from damages.
RY
בְּנֵר חֲנוּכָּה פָטוּר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא רְשׁוּת — he placed it outside with permission of the mitzvah, so he is exempt.
Ḥ
The Sages: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ חַיָיב — even when permitted, you remain liable for resulting damage.
Y
Parallel: people build sukkot in front of their stores on חג — that too is permitted, yet if a passerby is hurt, the builder pays.
The principle: Permission to perform an act (mitzvah or commercial) does not transfer the risk of damage onto the public. The actor still owns the consequences — except, per R' Yehudah, where the mitzvah itself authorizes the exposure.

📜 New Mishnah — Why כפל is Broader

The opening of chapter 7 contrasts the two penalty categories.
Mishnah: מְרוּבָּה מִידַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל

כפל is the default penalty for theft; ארבעה וחמשה is the narrow add-on for slaughter or sale of an ox or sheep.
Source-driven move: The Yerushalmi derives the breadth of כפל from אִם הִמָּצֵא תִמָּצֵא... חַיִּים — 'alive' includes all living things, 'the stolen goods' includes all movables.

Bottom line: The Torah's gnevah penalty fastens onto a movable, valuable, ownerless-once-stolen object. Anything that fails one of those tests drops out of the כפל system.
