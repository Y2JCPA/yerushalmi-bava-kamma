📖 Overview — A Long, Layered Daf

רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר. נַעֲשֶׂה כְּזוֹרֵק אֶת הַחֵץ מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם
The sugya: Daf 11 is dense: nine segments stretching across three Mishnayot. The opening unpacks the dog-with-hot-pitta case — Resh Lakish wants ignition contact at every sheaf; R' Yochanan rules נַעֲשֶׂה כְּזוֹרֵק אֶת הַחֵץ, and supporting beraitot test this against capital-vs-monetary liability. The middle of the daf moves to the tam/mu'ad Mishnah — R' Yehuda demands three days of goring, R' Meir three episodes — and the Yerushalmi explores what counts as continuity, paralleling a niddah dispute between Rav Ada bar Aḥawa and Rav Huna. Then comes the famous אָדָם מוּעָד לְעוֹלָם, with R' Yitzchak's narrowing: only when both were already sleeping. Finally the daf opens perek 3 with הַמַּנִּיחַ אֶת הַכַּד.

💬 The Dog and the Burning Sheaves

Why is full damage paid for the pitta but only half for the stack?
RL
בְּמַצִּית אֶת הָאוֹר עַל כָּל שִׁיבּוֹלֶת וְשִׁיבּוֹלֶת — only if the dog rekindled the fire on every single ear of grain.
R' Yo
נַעֲשֶׂה כְּזוֹרֵק אֶת הַחֵץ מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם — once the dog set the first ear ablaze, it is like an arrow shot from one place to another. The whole stack is treated as one act.
R' Yitz. b. Tev.
A beraita supports Resh Lakish: a kid bound to it, slave nearby = liable; slave bound, kid nearby = exempt. If R' Yochanan's arrow theory were right, the first ear would carry capital liability — and the rest would still be monetary, contradicting the beraita.
R' Yose
Same point from Shabbat: an ox lighting a stack on Shabbat = its owner pays; a person lighting it on Shabbat = exempt (because of capital liability). Without the arrow theory, monetary liability for later sheaves would still attach.
Yerushalmi style: The Yerushalmi probes the arrow-thrower theory by lining it up against capital-vs-monetary clashes. If the first ignition triggers a death penalty, all subsequent damage is absorbed into the original capital act.

🐂 The Gap-Day Question

If a bull gored, then skipped a day, then gored again — what is its status?
Day 1: gored. Day 2: not let out. Day 3: let out and gored.
Mu'ad or not?
↓
Yerushalmi: יָיבֹא כְתַפְלוּגְתָא דְּרַב אָדָא בַּר אַחֲוָוא וּדְרַב הוּנָא
Treat it as the niddah dispute
↓
Rav Ada bar Aḥawa (in Rav's name)
נִידָּה וַודַּאי
Definitely niddah → analogously, definitely mu'ad
Rav Huna (in Rav's name)
נִדָּה סָפֵק
Doubtful → analogously, doubtful mu'ad
↓
Rav Huna: I was there at the start, the middle, and the end — Rav reversed himself, ending on סָפֵק. Rav Ada only heard the first answer (וַודַּאי).
Why the niddah parallel: Both cases ask: when there is a gap-day in the middle, do we treat the bookend evidence as a continuous record (וַודַּאי) or as broken (סָפֵק)? It is the same structural question, transferred from blood to goring.

👤 אָדָם מוּעָד לְעוֹלָם — The Mishnah's Sweep

And R' Yitzchak's surprising narrowing.
1️⃣ Mishnah: a person is always notorious — shogeg or mezid, awake or asleep.
↓
2️⃣ If he blinded another or broke vessels, he pays full damages.
↓
3️⃣ R' Yitzchak: מַתְנִיתָא בְּשֶׁהָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם יְשֵׁינִין — the Mishnah is when both were already sleeping side by side.
↓
4️⃣ But if one was sleeping first and the other came and lay down beside him, the incoming sleeper is the muad — he created the danger.
↓
5️⃣ Net rule: 'always muad' is broad, but liability shifts to whoever changed the situation.
Bottom line: Even אָדָם מוּעָד לְעוֹלָם has a structural ceiling: when one person joins another already at rest, the joining party owns the risk.

🏺 Closing the Daf — The Jar in the Public Domain (3:1)

The daf opens perek 3 with a paradigmatic case.

🚶 The walker stumbles

  • נִתְקַל בָּהּ וּשְׁבָרָהּ — פָּטוּר
  • He breaks the jar by tripping → exempt
  • He cannot be sued for damaging the obstacle

🤕 The walker is hurt

  • וְאִם הוּזַּק בָּהּ בַּעַל הֶחָבִית חַיָיב בִּנְזָקָיו
  • Jar-owner pays for the walker's injury
  • He created a hazard in the public domain

🪨 Compare: hot water bottle case

  • Earlier in the daf: a man set out a פֻּלַטֵירָה in the open
  • A donkey passed and broke it
  • R' Yitzchak b. Tevelai: לֹא חַיָיב לָךְ כְּלוּם — and if the donkey was hurt, he pays!
The shared logic: Whoever places an object in the public domain bears the risk of what happens to it and what it does to others. The Mishnah of הַמַּנִּיחַ אֶת הַכַּד codifies what R' Yitzchak b. Tevelai already ruled in the practical case.
