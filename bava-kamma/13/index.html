📖 Overview — Pits, Shards, and Joshua's Condition

מִילְּתֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמְרָה. בּוֹר שֶׁלַּנִּזָּקִין פָּטוּר עַל הַכֵּלִים
The sugya: The daf opens with a sequence of stone-stumble cases that ask how far liability extends when a hazard is moved by wind or by a victim's own foot. R' Yochanan then states a foundational rule: בור שלנזקין פטור על הכלים — a בור-style hazard does not generate liability for damaged vessels, only for personal injury. The new Mishnah (3:2) adds the broken-jug case (slipping on water, cutting on shards) and R' Yehudah's distinction by intent. The Halakhah lays out R' Lazar / R' Yochanan's dispute about when exactly the dispute applies, and grounds the very right to leave damaged property in the road in the foundational principle that על מנת כן הנחיל יהושע לישראל את הארץ.

🪨 Three Stumble-Stone Cases

The Yerushalmi probes how far the chain of causation can stretch.
1️⃣ Two stones in רה"ר. A pedestrian stumbles on the first and is struck by the second. Who pays — first owner or second?
↓
2️⃣ A stone is placed in רה"ר. The wind blows it into a private domain; someone trips. Is the original owner liable?
↓
3️⃣ A stone is placed in רה"ר. Someone kicks it (נתקל בה והתיזה) into a private domain where a third person trips. Liability of the original owner?
↓
🔍 The Yerushalmi answers with the bull-pit baraita: when two causes converge, the rules of apportionment from Daf 12 carry over.
↓
🎯 The deeper issue: every stumble-stone is a בור — the question is whether the original creator stays liable once nature or another person displaces it.
Big picture: These three cases set up the next chiddush: even when liability does follow the stone, the pit-rules still cap it — vessels are not covered.

🍶 R' Yochanan: The Pit Pays for the Person, Not the Flask

A landmark formulation that will guide the rest of the chapter.
הִנִּיחַ אַבְנוֹ וּמַשָּׂאוֹ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים
Stone + load in the road. Pedestrian stumbles, holding a flask.
↓
Personal injury
חַיָיב עַל נִיזְקֵי אָדָם
Liable for damage to the person
Flask (כלים)
פָּטוּר עַל נִיזְקֵי צְלוּחִיתוֹ
Exempt for damage to the flask
↓
בּוֹר שֶׁלַּנִּזָּקִין פָּטוּר עַל הַכֵּלִים
A בור hazard does not generate liability for vessel damage.
↓
Exception: אִם כְּדֶרֶךְ הַטָּחָה הִטִּיחַ — אֲפִילוּ עַל נִיזְקֵי צְלוּחִית חַיָיב
If the impact was active 'walking' damage, even the flask is covered.
Source of the rule: Torah's pit verse (וְנָפַל שָׁמָּה שּׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר) names animals — the gemara reads this as excluding כלים. R' Yochanan converts this exegetical move into a working halakhic principle.

💧 The Broken-Jug Mishnah (3:2)

Two ways to be hurt by your neighbor's broken vessel — and a Tannaitic split on intent.

📜 Mishnah baseline

  • נִשְׁבְּרָה כַדּוֹ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים
  • Slips on the water → liable
  • Cut by the shards → liable

🟦 חכמים

  • Hold the owner liable in both modes
  • It is your hazard, your bill
  • No distinction by intent

🟥 ר' יהודה

  • בְּמִתְכַּוֵּן חַיָּיב, בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִתְכַּוֵּן פָּטוּר
  • Liable only if intentional
  • Reads ownership as ending when accident occurs
Why intent matters: R' Yehudah holds that אָדָם מַבְקִיר נְזָקָיו בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — a person is presumed to have abandoned (made hefker) his damaged property in the road. Without intent to harm, no liability.

💬 R' Lazar vs. R' Yochanan — When Is the Dispute?

Pinpointing the moment R' Yehudah and the Sages disagree.
RL
בִּשְׁעַת נְפִילָה נֶחְלְקוּ — they disagree only at the moment the jug is falling. Afterward, all agree he is liable.
RY
בֵּין בִּשְׁעַת נְפִילָה בֵּין לְאַחַר נְפִילָה הִיא הַמַּחֲלוֹקֶת — the dispute applies in both phases.
RY
Objection: if R' Yehudah exempts at the moment of falling, isn't it a קל וחומר that he exempts afterward too?
RL
He is exempt at the moment of falling because אָדָם מַבְקִיר נְזָקָיו בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — a person abandons his damaged goods. But afterward he has, in effect, re-claimed them.
Y
A baraita supports both readings. R' Zeira and R' La conclude: even R' Yehudah agrees that מַבְקִיר שׁוֹר שֶׁנָּגַח — one who declares his goring ox hefker — is still liable. לֹא דוֹמֶה נֶזֶק עוֹמֵד לְנֶזֶק מְהַלֵּךְ.
Conceptual takeaway: Stationary damage (broken jug shards) can be abandoned. Moving damage (a goring bull) cannot — because while it is in motion, it is still actively expressing its owner's neglect.

🌍 'Joshua's Condition' and the Pouring-Water Mishnah (3:3)

The deep reason why some public-domain damage is exempt.
SourceStatementWhat it teaches
Baraita cited by Yerushalmiשֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן הִנְחִיל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הָאָרֶץJoshua distributed Eretz Yisrael with a built-in clause permitting incidental public-domain use
Inferenceהָא אִם לֹא הִנְחִיל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ... עַל מְנָת כֵּן חַיָיבWithout that condition, the owner of any abandoned hazard would always be liable
Mishnah 3:3הַשּׁוֹפֵךְ מַיִם בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְהוּזַּק בָּהֶן אַחֵר חַיָיבPouring water is an active act, not passive abandonment — full liability
Rav Huna vs. RavRav Huna: liable for soiled garments; Rav: even soiled garments — exemptRav: בּוֹר שֶׁלַּנְּזָקִין פָּטוּר עַל הַכֵּלִים — same rule as R' Yochanan
Bringing it together: The whole liability scheme of Chapter 3 rests on two layers: (1) Joshua's condition limits the default liability for ordinary public-domain mishaps; (2) within that, the בור-vs-מהלך distinction decides whether vessels are covered. R' Yochanan and Rav agree on the bottom line — pit-style damage spares כלים.
