📖 Overview — Damage Math, Sheaves, and Fire

אֵין שָׁמִין בֵּית כּוֹר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא פוֹגֵם. וְלֹא בֵית קַב מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּשְׁבִּיחַ. אֶלָּא שָׁמִין בֵּית סְאָה
The sugya: The daf finishes the grazing-damages math (why you don't measure by an unfair-large or unfair-small parcel), surveys R' Akiva's adjustments for unripe vs. mature produce, and pivots to Mishnah 6:3's neat reversal: stack sheaves without permission and the field-owner's animal eats them — you swallow the loss; stack with permission and the owner has to pay if his animal damages them. Then comes perek 6:4 — fire. The Mishnah builds a chain of liability through deaf-mutes, agents, woodcarriers, and finally the wind itself.

📐 Why a Bet Se'ah Is the Right Yardstick

The Tanna's middle path between fairness extremes.
Parcel SizeProblemVerdict
בֵּית כּוֹרToo big — averages out the damage, victim under-compensated✗ פוגם
בֵּית קַבToo small — local damage looks worse than it is✗ משביח
בֵּית סְאָהRight balance — represents the surrounding field fairly✓ שָׁמִין
Yannai variations: Beit Yannai: שָׁמִין תַּרְקָב בְּשִׁשִׁים תַּרְקַבִּים — three-kav unit in 60 of them. R' Yose ben Chanina: קֶלַח בְּשִׁשִּׁים קְלָחִים — even one stalk in 60. Different scales of the same averaging principle.

🍇 R' Akiva's Tiers — Pricing the Damage

Different produce stages get different valuations.

🌱 Shoots / לולבי גפנים

  • Ate fig shoots or vine shoots
  • Estimated by current shoot value
  • R' Yehudah: this is the bet-se'ah case

🍇 Unripe / בוסר ופגין

  • Ate unripe fruit
  • Estimated as fully ripe
  • Loss is the harvest the owner would have had

🌳 Mature trees / נטיעות

  • R' Yehudah b/n R' Akiva: damage by saplings
  • נְטִיעוֹת שָׁמִין לוֹ נְטִיעוֹת
  • Trees valued by tree, not by produce
Sharper rule: R' Shimon ben Yehudah: תְּבוּאָה שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיאָה שְׁלִישׁ נִידּוֹנֶת כִּנְטִיעוֹת — grain that hasn't reached one-third growth is treated like saplings, not produce.

🌾 The Sheaves Mishnah — Permission Reverses Liability

Same physical situation, opposite legal results.
You stacked sheaves on someone else's field
↓
Without permission
Field-owner's animal ate them → he is פטור. Your sheaves were the trespass.
With permission
Field-owner's animal ate them → he is חייב. He invited the sheaves; he owes shemira.
↓
R' Yitzchak's question: Are sheaves on a field treated like vessels in a house?
↓
R' Yose b. R' Bun: only when the field אִית לֵיהּ מִסְגָּר — has a closure/fence
Symbolic pivot: Permission isn't a polite formality — it's the moment legal responsibility shifts. Without it you're a trespasser; with it the owner becomes the shomer.

🔥 The Fire Mishnah — Chain of Causation

Each link in the chain shifts liability to whoever made it dangerous.
1️⃣ Sent through a חרש שוטה וקטן → exempt בדיני אדם, חייב בדיני שמים.
↓
2️⃣ Sent through a competent person → that person is liable.
↓
3️⃣ One brought the fire, another the wood → whoever brought the wood is liable.
↓
4️⃣ One brought wood, another the fire → whoever brought the fire is liable.
↓
5️⃣ A third person fanned the flames → that person is liable. The wind fanned it → all are exempt.
The principle: Liability follows the active completing cause — whoever turns potential into damage takes the loss. Once nature (the wind) takes over, the chain breaks.

💨 What Kind of Wind Breaks the Chain?

Catastrophic vs. ordinary, and the implications.
Tamman
The Mishnah's exemption is for רוּחַ שֶׁלְּאוֹנְסִין — catastrophic wind. Normal wind keeps you liable.
R' Y / RL
Even normal wind: אֲפִילוּ רוּחַ שֶׁהָעוֹלָם מִתְנָהֵג בּוֹ פָּטוּר. Why? שֶׁפְּעָמִים בָּא פְּעָמִים לֹא בָא — wind isn't predictable enough to count as your direct extension.
Chizkiyah
Sub-debate: Did you hand him a גחלת (coal) or a שלהבת (flame)? A coal can fizzle out — flame is unstoppable.
R' Yochanan
הִיא גַחֶלֶת הִיא שַׁלְהֶבֶת — same thing.
What's at stake: How predictable does the spread mechanism need to be for it to count as your fire? The stricter view (Tamman) treats normal weather as your tool. The lenient view treats unpredictability as a break in the chain.
