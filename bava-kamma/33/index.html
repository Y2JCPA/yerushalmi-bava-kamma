📖 Overview — Stolen Sales, Sneaky Slaughters, and the Settlement

אָמַר רִבִּי לָעְזָר. רָאוּ אוֹתוֹ שׁוֹחֵט שׁוֹר שָׁחוֹר בַּיַּעַר חֲזָקָה גָּנוּב הוּא
The sugya: The daf closes out perek 7 of Bava Kamma. It refines the Mishnah's rules on ארבעה וחמישה: what counts as a complete sale, what counts as a complete slaughter, and exactly when the thief's act becomes binding. From there the Yerushalmi turns to the very different — but related — world of where animals may be raised: the bans on small livestock, chickens, pigs, and unchained dogs in Eretz Yisrael, and where one may set pigeon traps.

🌲 The Black Ox in the Forest

R' Lazar's chazakah of theft and the Yerushalmi's challenge.
RL
רָאוּ אוֹתוֹ שׁוֹחֵט שׁוֹר שָׁחוֹר בַּיַּעַר חֲזָקָה גָּנוּב הוּא — slaughtering a black ox in the forest is presumptive evidence of theft.
Q
But maybe the ox was already lying down? A passerby could simply slaughter an animal he found resting.
A
תִּיפְתָּר בְּשֶׁרָאוּהוּ מוֹשֵׁךְ — the ruling speaks of a case where they saw him dragging it. Then the chazakah of theft holds.
Reading the move: The Yerushalmi tightens R' Lazar's principle by adding a behavioral fingerprint: a thief drags the animal out of sight before slaughtering. The pasture context plus the dragging is what triggers the chazakah.

🔪 Slaughterer vs. Seller

Each act has a feature the other lacks.

🔪 הטובח

  • הַטּוֹבֵחַ בַּשַׁבָּת חַיָיב
  • Slaughtering is a melachah on Shabbat
  • Carries criminal liability

💰 המוכר

  • וְהַמּוֹכֵר בַּשַׁבָּת פָּטוּר
  • Sale is not in itself a melachah
  • But still completes the kinyan for ד׳ וה׳

🌱 R' Yannai's edge case

  • ‘תְּלוֹשׁ מִן הַקַּרְקַע הַזֶּה וּקְנֵה אוֹתוֹ’
  • Sale + tearing from the ground
  • R' Yose b. R' Bun: it's the tearing, not the sale, that creates the criminal liability
Why the comparison: The Yerushalmi is mapping the unique halakhic profile of each act so neither can simply be derived from the other — the same logical move it made earlier between שור and בור.

🚪 When Does the Thief Become Liable for ד׳ וה׳?

The Mishnah of הָיָה מוֹשְׁכוֹ וְיוֹצֵא.
Thief is moving the stolen animal — and it dies en route
↓
Drawing it (still on owner's property)
פָּטוּר
Kinyan not yet complete
Lifted it (הגביהו)
חַיָיב
הגבהה is universal kinyan
Got it off the property
חַיָיב
Removal completes the theft
↓
R' Mana's question: does פָּטוּר here mean exempt from ד׳ וה׳ — or exempt even from the basic theft itself?
↓
The Mishnah reaches the same result whether the thief acts personally or hands it to a שומר / לוקח / שואל — the kinyan-act is what counts.
Key insight: ד׳ וה׳ is gated by a completed kinyan of theft. הגבהה or removal seal it; mere drawing on the owner's premises does not.

🐑 The Final Mishnah: Where Animals May Be Raised

A sharp pivot from theft into community-protective halakhot.
1️⃣ בהמה דקה: forbidden in Eretz Yisrael — but permitted in Syria and in מדברות שבארץ ישראל (R' Abba: e.g. Mahir, 16×16 mil).
↓
2️⃣ תרנגולין in Yerushalayim: forbidden because of the קדשים they would defile by digging up impure things.
↓
3️⃣ Cohanim anywhere in EY: forbidden, because of the טהרות they handle.
↓
4️⃣ חזירים: a Jew may not raise pigs anywhere — full stop.
↓
5️⃣ כלב: only permitted if קָשׁוּר בַּשַׁלְשֶׁלֶת; נשבים ליונים only 30 ris from any settlement.
R' Yehoshua b. Levi's lens: He ties the chicken ban to יְרוּשָׁלַ͏ִם הַבְּנוּיָה — עִיר שֶׁמְחַבֶּרֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל זֶה לָזֶה. Yerushalayim is a city built around shared sanctity, so its rules protect that fabric.
