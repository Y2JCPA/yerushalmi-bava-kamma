📖 Overview — Capacity, Custodians, and the Bull That Killed

שׁוֹר שֶׁל חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן שֶׁנָּגחוּ מַעֲמִידִין לָהֶן אֶפִּיטְרוֹפּוֹס וּמְעִידִין בָּהֶן בִּפְנֵי אֶפִּיטְרוֹפּוֹס
The sugya: The daf works through the Mishnah of an ox owned by a חרש, שוטה, or קטן — people without halakhic agency. A guardian (אפיטרופוס) is appointed so testimony can establish notoriety. The Yerushalmi presses: what if the ox was warned in front of the guardian and then returned to the owner? What about a borrowed ox that turns out to be mu'ad? It then asks the deeper question of whether guardians ever take on personal liability, and closes with the Mishnah of an ox that gored and killed a person — kofer for mu'ad, none for tam, but execution either way.

💬 Do We Appoint Guardians To Their Detriment?

R' Yochanan vs. R' Yose b. Chaninah on guardian liability.
RY
R' Yochanan: בַּתְּחִילָּה אֵין מְמַנִּין אֶפִּיטְרוֹפִּין עַל מְנָת לַחוֹב לָהֶן אֶלָּא לְזַכּוֹת לָהֶן — guardians are appointed only to benefit, but if their actions hurt, they hurt.
RYC
R' Yose b. Chaninah: never — neither initially nor afterward. Guardians can neither help nor harm the orphans' rights.
Q
But our Mishnah appoints a guardian — that very appointment harms (it sets up testimony that will impose liability)?
A
Answer: שַׁנְיָיא הִיא בְּשׁוֹר שֶׁלֹּא יֵצֵא וְיַזִּיק — an ox is different; left alone it will keep damaging, so the appointment is unavoidable.
Bottom line: Guardian appointment is normally restricted to benefit. The dangerous-ox case is a public-safety exception that overrides the rule.

🏚️ Whose Pocket Pays?

If the orphans' bull damages, who absorbs the loss?

👤 R' Yochanan

  • Pay from the orphans' property
  • Reasoning: if the guardian had to pay, לֹא נַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם מֵעוֹלָם אֶפִּיטְרוֹפּוֹס
  • Aligns with the Rabbis: court-appointed guardian swears (court already vetted him)

👥 R' Yose b. Chaninah

  • Pay from the guardian's property
  • Aligns with Abba Shaul
  • Logic: a person will sacrifice much to be called נאמן (trustworthy)

🪙 The תוספת

  • Abba bar Rav Huna: one who hits another's animal that then damages — liable
  • Strengthens the chain: indirect causation still attaches
  • Reinforces the case for guardian-side liability
Practical concern: R' Yochanan worries about the supply of guardians — if liability is too steep, no one will serve. R' Yose b. Chaninah worries about deterrence and incentives — guardians must guard.

🔁 The Borrowed-Ox Liability Split

An elegant case-by-case ruling embedded in the sugya.
1️⃣ A borrows an ox from B believing it is תם.
↓
2️⃣ The ox damages — turns out it was already מועד.
↓
3️⃣ Default ruling: הַבְּעָלִין מְשַׁלְּמִין חֲצִי נֶזֶק וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
↓
4️⃣ R' Eleazar: this can only be R' Yose's view, since R' Yose says הֲרֵי הוּא בְּחֶזְקָתוֹ.
↓
5️⃣ Refinement: if the borrower knew the ox was a גocer, full liability falls on him.
The elegance: The sugya splits liability cleanly: half from the original mu'ad status (owner), half from the act of borrowing it (borrower). Knowledge of the truth shifts everything onto the knowing party.
