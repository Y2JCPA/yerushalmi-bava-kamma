📖 Overview — Mishmar Disputes and Inheriting Stolen Goods

רִבִּי חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר. בִּשְׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת פְּלִיגִין. רִבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר. בְּשַׁבָּת אַחַת
The sugya: The first half of the daf parses the Mishnah's mishmar rule: R' Ḥizqiah holds the dispute is about two consecutive weeks, R' Yose holds it is about a single week. From this emerges a principle: a sacrifice given to a watch during their week stays with them, but a sacrifice given out of week — or sat on through laziness — can be removed. The daf then opens chapter 10 with the question of children who inherit stolen goods. Tannaim teach the heirs are exempt, but if they inherit a 'דבר שיש בו אחריות' they must pay. R' Yonatan, Reish Lakish, Rav, and Shmuel debate what that phrase covers — real estate, the actual stolen item, or both.

💬 R' Ḥizqiah vs. R' Yose on the Mishmar Mishnah

Are the parties disputing a two-week or one-week scenario?
RḤ
בִּשְׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת פְּלִיגִין — money to Yehoiarib in Yedaiah's week, asham to Yedaiah in Yehoiarib's week: he has fulfilled his obligation.
RY
בְּשַׁבָּת אַחַת — same scenario, but he has not fulfilled his obligation.
Rebbi
Following R' Yehudah: if Yehoiarib's men offered the asham, no atonement; the money must be transferred from Yedaiah to Yehoiarib so they offer it properly.
Y
זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת — this implies: a sacrifice given to a watch not in their week can be removed from their hand.
The hidden principle: The two-week reading exposes a rule the one-week reading hides: ownership of a sacrifice depends on receiving it during one's own watch.

🏛️ What Counts as 'דבר שיש בו אחריות'?

Four Amoraic positions on which inherited assets trigger liability.

🌍 R' Yonatan

  • בְּשֶׁהִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶן קַרְקַע
  • Liability only if heirs inherit real estate
  • Real estate is the classic 'אחריות' asset

👕 Reish Lakish

  • בְשֶׁהִנִּיחַ לִפְנֵיהֶן גּוּף הַגְּזֵילָה
  • Only when the actual stolen item is in their hands
  • If the object exists, it must be returned

📜 Rav

  • יוֹרֵשׁ כִּמְשׁוּעְבָּד
  • An heir is like a holder of encumbered property
  • Loan-by-witnesses cannot collect from him

⚖️ Shmuel

  • דְּאִיקנֵי אֵינוֹ גוֹבֶה מִמְּשׁוּעְבָּדִין
  • After-acquired collateral: not from encumbered, yes from בני חרי
  • Mishnah fits if heir got גוף הגזילה
The Yerushalmi's resolution: וְלֵית הִיא פְלִיגָא — neither Rav nor Shmuel is contradicted by the Mishnah. Each fits a different inheritance scenario: Rav with real estate, Shmuel with the actual stolen item.

👕 Robbed Tallit Passed to a Third Party

Can it be reclaimed from the second holder?
1️⃣ Setup: גָּזַל טַלִּית וּנְתָנָהּ לְאַחֵר — A robs a tallit, gives it to B.
↓
2️⃣ R' Eleazar in the name of R' Ḥiyya: מוֹצִיאִין מֵרִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא מִשֵּׁינִי — recover from A, not from B.
↓
3️⃣ R' Yoḥanan in the name of R' Yannai: מוֹצִיאִין אַף מִן הַשֵּׁינִי — recover even from B.
↓
4️⃣ R' Abba bar Mamal: even R' Ḥiyya himself agreed — he too holds מוציאין אף משיני.
↓
5️⃣ Bottom line: the stolen tallit can be reclaimed from both the original robber and the recipient.
The deeper question: This is the same machloket as inheriting גוף הגזילה — does the physical object still carry the original owner's claim once it has moved hands? The Yerushalmi leans toward: yes, it does.
