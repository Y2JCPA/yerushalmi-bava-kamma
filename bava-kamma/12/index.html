📖 Overview — When a Jug Becomes a Pit

הַמַּנִּיחַ אֶת הַכַּד בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים... אִם יִטְלֶינָּהּ מִיכָּאן וְיִתְנֶינָּהּ כָּאן הֲרֵי זֶה בוֹר
The sugya: The Mishnah's case of המניח את הכד ברשות הרבים opens a chain of questions about when a stationary object becomes a בור for which its owner is liable. Rav, Shmuel and R' Lazar map out the conditions: filling the public domain, sitting at a corner, or being moved at all. The Yerushalmi then anchors this with the bull-mounting baraita — pushing vs. allowing — and uses R' Yose's striking parallels (irrigation flood, Roman tax collector, billeted soldiers) to formulate a principle of timely intervention. The daf closes with the apportionment dispute when an attacking ox knocks another into a pit.

🏺 Three Views on the Jug in Public Domain

When does putting down a vessel make its owner liable as a בור?

🟦 רב

  • Liable only בִּמְמַלָּא אֶת כָּל רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים
  • If the jug fills the entire public road
  • Otherwise: it is normal to put things there

🟪 שמואל

  • Liable if it fills the road or
  • נְתוּנָה עַל קֶרֶן זָוִית — placed at a corner
  • Adds the corner case as a hazard

🟩 רבי לעזר

  • Even if it does not fill the road
  • Even if not at a corner
  • אֵין דֶּרֶךְ אָדָם לְהָנִיחָן בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים
Trajectory: From narrow (Rav) to broad (R' Lazar). The wider you read, the more often a stationary object counts as a בור.

🪜 The Trap of Picking It Up

If your jug is in the road and you move it, have you just dug a pit?
הָיְתָה מְמַלָּא כָּל רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים
Your jug fills the road
↓
אִם יִטְלֶינָּהּ מִיכָּן וְיִתְנֶינָּהּ כָּאן נַעֲשֶׂה בוֹר
Moving it from here to there creates a בור
↓
Option A
יִטּוֹל הַמַּקֵּל וִישַׁבְּרֶנָּהּ
Take a stick and break it
Option B
יַעֲבוֹר עָלֶיהָ וְאִם נִשְׁבְּרָה נִשְׁבְּרָה
Step over it; if it breaks, it breaks
↓
Better to destroy your own property than to relocate the hazard onto a new spot.
Key intuition: An object you abandon in the public domain is not just sitting there — it is a hazard you are still responsible for. Moving it does not solve the problem; it just shifts the בור.

💬 The Bull-Mounting Proof

The Yerushalmi imports an animal-damage baraita to settle the jug case.
Y
נִישְׁמְעִינָהּ מִן הָדָא — let us learn from this baraita.
B
שׁוֹר שֶׁעָלָה עַל חֲבֵירוֹ — a bull mounted another. If the lower owner pulled his out, or it slipped away, and the attacker fell and died → פטור.
B
אִם דְּחָייוֹ וְנָפַל וָמֵת — חַיָיב. If he pushed the attacker, he must pay.
Q
Couldn't the attacker's owner say, 'If you had not interfered, I would have paid your damages anyway'?
A
He could answer back: 'If I had not interfered, you would have paid me only חצי נזק (the bull was תם)!' And if it was מועד — 'I'm not going to chase you through the courts.'
Why this matters for the jug: Both cases share the same logic — once your property is damaging or being damaged, you have a right to protect yourself, but only by passive removal. Active pushing creates new liability.

🚿 R' Yose's Three Parallels — Timing Is Everything

When the threat has not yet arrived, you may divert it. Once it arrives, you may not.
1️⃣ Irrigation flood: רָאָה אַמַּת הַמַּיִם שׁוֹטֶפֶת וּבָאָה לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ — before the water reaches your field, you may divert it elsewhere. Once it has reached, you may not.
↓
2️⃣ Chrysargyros (Roman tax): before the collector arrives, you may say 'X is in my profession, Y is in my profession' — directing the bill to others. Once he arrives, forbidden.
↓
3️⃣ Roman billet: before the soldiers arrive in town, you may bribe the quartermaster to send them elsewhere. Once they arrive, forbidden.
↓
🪞 The unifying rule: damage that is still approaching may be redirected. Damage that has landed on someone may not be transferred to another.
↓
🎯 R' Yose reads this principle straight back into the jug case: once your jug is broken, you cannot relocate the hazard onto a new neighbor.
Yerushalmi style: Three vivid real-world scenarios — irrigation, taxation, military quartering — woven into a halakhic principle. The Yerushalmi loves to anchor abstractions in lived experience.

📊 Ox Pushes Ox Into a Pit — How to Split the Bill

When two damagers act together, the Tannaim disagree on apportionment.
PositionCase: מועדCase: תם
Anonymous baraitaבַּעַל הַשּׁוֹר חַיָיב; pit owner exemptSame: ox owner pays full
רבי נתןEach pays מֶחֱצָה — half eachOx owner: שני חלקים; pit owner: רביע
רבי חנינה (correcting)—Ox owner: שלשה חלקים; pit owner: רביע
Underlying logicMu'ad pays full → so split full damageTam pays half → ox owner only chayav for his half-share, but pit still creates extra exposure
The arithmetic puzzle: R' Chanania reads the baraita's numbers as fractions of full damage: ox owner pays 3/4 (since tam pays 1/2 of his portion of two-thirds), pit owner pays 1/4. The point is that even when the immediate cause is the bull, the pit's existence still creates real liability.
