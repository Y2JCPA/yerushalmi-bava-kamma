📖 Overview — Hazards Left in the Public Domain

כָּל הַמַּזִּיק שֶׁלֹּא עַל יְדֵי מַעֲשֶׂה חַיָיב ... וְכָל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה
The sugya: The end of perek 3 takes up new pit-like hazards: thorns sticking out of a fence, a fallen wall, and dung or straw left in the road. R' Yochanan reads the thorn case as one where the thorns 'protrude outward' (מפריח). R' Meir holds you can be liable for damage by inaction, but only after the 30-day deadline. The Mishnah then introduces a fascinating side door: anyone who beats you to the leftover straw or dung can acquire it — which sends the Yerushalmi into a parallel discussion about how movables are actually transferred (turning, dragging, lifting).

🌵 Reading 'He Fenced With Thorns'

R' Yochanan vs. R' Yose b. R' Yehudah on what makes the case work for everyone.
R' Y
תִּיפְתָּר דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בְּמַפְרִיחַ — read the case as one where the thorns protrude outward, into the public domain. Then everyone agrees there is liability.
R' Bun
Why not just read it like R' Yose b. R' Yehudah, who says שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁהֵן סְמוּכִין לִרְשׁוּת כִּרְשׁוּת — three tefachim adjacent to a domain count as the domain itself?
R' Z
Aren't you satisfied with R' Yochanan's universal reading? His move makes the Mishnah work for every Tanna, not just one.
Why this matters: The Yerushalmi prefers the reading that doesn't depend on a single Tanna's view. מפריח turns the case into a clear public-domain hazard rather than a borderline three-tefach question.

🧱 A Fallen Wall — Damage Without an Act

R' Meir's principle, sharpened by Reish Lakish.
כָּל הַמַּזִּיק שֶׁלֹּא עַל יְדֵי מַעֲשֶׂה חַיָיב
Reish Lakish's claim: The case of a wall that collapsed into the public domain is specifically R' Meir's view. R' Meir holds: anyone whose property causes damage — even when the owner did nothing — is liable. This is the same principle that grounds the entire pit framework: a hazard you created by neglect is still a hazard you own.
The contrast: Other Tannaim require an affirmative act of damage. R' Meir alone extends liability to the owner who simply failed to remove a known risk.

📅 The 30-Day Deadline

When does negligence start to count?
1️⃣ A tree leans dangerously, or a wall begins to crack.
↓
2️⃣ Beit Din gives the owner time to cut down or take it down.
↓
3️⃣ Within the deadline, the structure falls and damages someone — owner is פָּטוּר.
↓
4️⃣ After the deadline, the same fall now triggers full liability — חַיָיב.
↓
5️⃣ R' Hoshaya: the deadline is 30 days. תַּנֵּי רִבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָה. ל׳ יוֹם.
The legal logic: Inaction only becomes liability once the owner had reasonable time to act. The 30-day window is not a grace period — it's the line where negligence becomes legally cognizable.

🪵 Straw, Olive-Press, and Dung in the Road

The Mishnah introduces a counter-intuitive rule.

🌾 Straw / גפת

  • Owner takes them out לזבלים — to compost
  • Causes damage to a passerby → owner liable
  • Anyone can grab them: וְכָל הַקּוֹדֵם בָּהֶן זָכָה

💩 Dung / גלל

  • Owner turns it over in the road
  • Damage to passerby → owner liable
  • Same acquisition rule applies

⚖️ The Paradox

  • Owner stays liable for damage
  • But loses ownership to whoever grabs it first
  • Hazard ≠ Possession
The two regimes: Tort liability and property ownership run on different tracks. The owner's liability follows the act of placing a hazard; ownership of the object itself is forfeited the moment another person takes it.

🤲 How Are Movables Acquired?

Hizkiyah opens a side door — turning over to acquire.
MethodWhat It IsWhen It Works
הֲפִיכָהTurning the object overHizkiyah: only if done with intent to acquire
גְּרִירָהDragging the objectR' Bun bar Chiyya: works for pillars and stiff hides
הַגְבָּהָהLifting the objectRequired for soft hides — dragging won't do it
שְׁבָחָן / גּוּפָןWhat is acquiredBeit Yannai: only the appreciation; Shmuel: the object itself
The deeper move: The Yerushalmi turns a Mishnah about hazards into a window onto kinyan theory. The physical actions that mark ownership transfer depend on the nature of the object — and that interpretive lens then helps us understand what 'first to grab it acquires it' really means.
