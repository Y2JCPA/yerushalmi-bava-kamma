📖 Overview — How Far Does Fire Travel?

תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר אוֹ הַשָּׂדֶה ... רוֹאִין אוֹתָהּ כְּאִילּוּ הִיא בְּאֶמְצַע בֵּית כּוֹר
The sugya: The daf walks through the Torah's strange list of fire-damage objects (kotzim, gadish, kamah, sadeh) and shows how each word stops you from over-restricting the others. Then comes the geography: a wall four amos high, a road, or a river — does the fire-setter still pay if his fire crossed those? R' Abba and R' Yochanan split on whether the Mishnah is about a 'burning' fire or a 'wind-whipped' fire. Then Mishnah 6:6's distance debate (16, 50, or 'bet kor center'), capped by the famous Mishnah of the goat tied to the stack and the slave standing nearby — where capital crime exempts payment.

📜 Why Four Words for Fire Targets?

Each Torah term blocks a different over-restriction.
WordWhat it addsWithout it I'd say...
קוֹצִיםFlammable items—
גָּדִישׁHarvested materialOnly in-ground items
קָמָהStanding growing cropsOnly harvested items
אוֹ הַשָּׂדֶהLand itself — even non-flammable damageOnly flammable; not the field, furrow, or stones
Why all four: Without 'sadeh,' you'd exempt damage to bare furrow or stone. Without 'kamah,' you'd exempt growing crops. The Yerushalmi shows each word covers a gap — and asks how Chachamim, who limit liability to wheat/barley stacks, square that with the word 'sadeh.'

💬 The Wall, the Road, the River

When does a fire's leap break the chain?
M
עָבְרָה גָדֵר שֶׁהוּא גָבוֹהַּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת אוֹ דֶרֶךְ הָרַבִּים אוֹ נָהָר פָּטוּר — crossed a 4-amos wall, road, or river → exempt.
R' Abba
The Mishnah is about קוֹדַחַת — a burning fire. But a wind-whipped fire (מְקַטֶּפֶת) — everyone agrees you're liable, because the wind extends your causation.
R' Y
Inverse: the Mishnah is about מְקַטֶּפֶת. A burning fire (קוֹדַחַת) — exempt, because steady fire stays put.
Behind the dispute: Whether the human's fire-act extends through wind. R' Abba treats wind as your tool; R' Yochanan treats it as a break. Either way the wall/road/river is the geographical limit case.

📏 The Distance Debate — Mishnah 6:6

How far does fire have to travel to count?

🌐 R' Eleazar b. Azaryah

  • Imagine the fire at the center of a bet kor
  • Liability based on whether it could reasonably reach there
  • Largest scope

🛣️ R' Eliezer

  • 16 amos, like a public road
  • בִּשְׁעַת הָרוּחַ: 30 amos
  • Tied to standard road width

📐 R' Akiva

  • 50 amos
  • With wind: 100 amos
  • Stricter on the fire-setter

⚖️ R' Shimon

  • הַכֹּל לְפִי הַדְּלֵיקָה
  • Case-by-case based on the fire's reality
  • No fixed number
From the case file: מַעֲשֶׂה בָּעֲרָב שֶׁקָּפַץ הָאוֹר יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת אַמָּה — in Arabia a fire jumped over 300 amos. The numbers in the Mishnah cap normal liability; extreme fires need R' Shimon's flexible rule. נָהָר אוֹ שְׁלוּלִית רְחָבִין שְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת ends liability.

🥬 Hidden Items in the Stack

The R' Yehudah / Chachamim debate runs in opposite directions.
Burned a stack with hidden כלים
↓
R' Yehudah
יְשַׁלֵּם כָּל מַה שֶׁבְּתוֹכוֹ — pays for everything inside
Chachamim
אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא גָדִישׁ חִטִּים אוֹ שְׂעוֹרִים — only the wheat/barley stack
↓
BUT — Chachamim agree with R' Yehudah on threshing-floor implements (כלי גורן) since they're normally hidden there
↓
And both sides agree: burning a building → pays for everything inside — since storage is normal in homes
R' Hoshaya's harmonization: When there are no witnesses to what was hidden, the dispute remains. But with witnesses, all agree the victim collects per the proven contents — even by oath in cases of disputed sack contents.

🐐 The Kafut Goat and the Nearby Slave

Where capital liability swallows monetary liability.
1️⃣ Goat tied to the stack, slave nearby: both burn → fire-setter חַיָיב.
↓
2️⃣ Slave tied to the stack, goat nearby: both burn → fire-setter פָּטוּר.
↓
3️⃣ Why the difference? In case 2, killing the slave is a capital crime.
↓
4️⃣ Principle: קַם לֵיהּ בְּדְרַבָּה מִינֵּיהּ — when one act triggers both capital and monetary liability, the capital absorbs the monetary.
↓
5️⃣ Result: the fire-setter walks free of even the goat's value, because his single act is on the death track.
Sharp consequence: The slave's life situation determines the goat's price tag. Halakhah doesn't subdivide one act of arson into separate liabilities — the heaviest consequence absorbs the rest.
