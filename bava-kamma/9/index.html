📖 Overview — Tooth, Foot, and Wings

רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר. עַל הָרִאשׁוֹנָה הוּשְּׁבָה. רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר. עַל כּוּלָּהּ הוּשְּׁבָה
The sugya: Daf 9 plays out three connected debates. First, Rav Huna distinguishes between a thread that tied itself onto a chicken's foot and one the owner tied — the second is full damage. Then a long beraita maps every chicken motion (pecking, scraping, falling, flying, blowing) onto full or half. Then Mishnah 2:3 opens the שן sugya, and Resh Lakish vs. R' Yochanan disagree whether the Sages' answer to R' Tarphon (הוּשְׁבָה) covers the whole Mishnah or only its first clause — a dispute that decides what happens when a walking animal kicks one lying down.

💬 Rav Huna on the Tied Thread

The Mishnah said half damage for a chicken with a thread on its foot. Rav Huna asks: who tied it?
M
הָיָה דְלִיל קָשׁוּר בְּרַגְלוֹ... מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק
Rav Huna
בְּשֶׁנִּקְשַׁר מֵאֵילָיו — only when the thread tied itself. אֲבָל אִם קְשָׁרוֹ הוּא — מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
Y
If the owner tied it, he turned a chicken into a hazard. The half-damage rule for keren-style abnormal motion no longer applies — he pays in full.
Why this matters: Rav Huna is locating the half-damage rule precisely: it's for accidents of nature, not for hazards a person creates. Once the owner ties the string, the chicken becomes a moving bor in his name.

🪣 The Falling Chicken — Why Not a בור?

R' Yose ben R' Bun fixes a tricky comparison.
נָפַל עַל חֲבֵירוֹ
Upper chicken pays full, lower pays half
↓
Objection: וְאֵינוֹ כְעוֹשֶׂה בוֹר בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד וּמַזִּיק בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר?
Isn't this like a pit dug here that damages there — which is exempt?
↓
R' Yose b. R' Bun: מִכֵּיוָן שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכּוֹ לִיפּוֹל אֶלָּא בְאוֹתוֹ הַבּוֹר כְּמִי שֶׁהוּא בוֹרוֹ
Since it only naturally falls into that pit, the pit becomes 'his'
The move: R' Yose collapses the bor problem by re-attributing the pit. The lower chicken did not dig; the upper chicken's natural fall makes the destination effectively his own bor — so liability holds.

💬 Resh Lakish vs. R' Yochanan on the Mishnah of שן

How much of Mishnah 2:3 did the Sages 'send back' (הוּשְׁבָה)?
RL
עַל הָרִאשׁוֹנָה הוּשְּׁבָה — only the first clause was answered. → A walking animal that kicks one lying down: exempt.
R' Yo
עַל כּוּלָּהּ הוּשְּׁבָה — the whole Mishnah was answered. → Same case: liable.
R' Immi
RL only exempts where a walking animal kicks a lying one. But lying-kicks-walking, or walking-kicks-walking — even RL holds liable.
R' Hoshaia
כּוּלָּהּ פָּטוּר — all such cases are exempt. אֵין קֶרֶן בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים? — there's no keren liability in רה"ר at all in this scenario!
Yerushalmi style: A whole halakhic landscape is unfolded from one word, הוּשְׁבָה. The reach of the Sages' reply decides who pays in every walking/lying combination.

🦷 שן vs. קרן: The Symmetry the Yerushalmi Builds

Rav and Ilfai use a parallel between leniency for tooth and stringency for horn.
1️⃣ שן (tooth): if the animal stood still and ate from a heap — חַיֶיבֶת (Rav).
↓
2️⃣ Leniency for שן: a walking animal eating from a standing heap — exempt.
↓
3️⃣ קרן (horn): mirror image — a walking animal that kicks a standing one is liable.
↓
4️⃣ Ilfai pushes further: even walking-kicks-walking is liable — keren is treated stringently across the board.
↓
5️⃣ Net rule: לעיכוב — שן is more forgiving by motion-state; קרן is harsher by motion-state.
Bottom line: The Yerushalmi builds the שן/קרן contrast as a structural symmetry: where one is lenient, the other is stringent — and motion (walking, lying, standing) is the diagnostic feature for both.
