📖 Overview — When and How Testimony Falls Apart

אָמַר רִבִּי זְעִירָא. הָדָא אָמְרָה. עָד זוֹמֵם אֵין נִפְסָל בְּבֵית דִּין אֶלָּא מֵעַצְמוֹ נִפְסָל
The sugya: The Halakhah probes when perjured witnesses are disqualified — at the moment of being exposed, or only by court ruling? R' Ze'ira holds it is automatic. R' Yose distinguishes between one testimony with one warning (where partial = total nullification) and two separate testimonies. Two scenarios show 'sequential' witnesses: a previously rejected pair can be retroactively accepted on the strength of the second pair. The new Mishnah (7:4) closes the page: one witness or self-confession on the slaughter side ⇒ כפל only.

💬 When is a Zomem Disqualified?

R' Ze'ira reads the Mishnah's rule about partial perjury.
RZ
עָד זוֹמֵם אֵין נִפְסָל בְּבֵית דִּין אֶלָּא מֵעַצְמוֹ נִפְסָל — a perjured witness disqualifies himself; the court does not need to act.
Q
Then how does the Mishnah's case (one of the second pair zomem ⇒ second testimony falls) work? If self-disqualification is automatic, no testimony was ever valid.
A
Read it as a case of warning (הַתְרָיָיה) — and bring R' Yose's brayta: one testimony + one warning = partial nullification triggers full nullification.
Why this matters: If perjury is self-executing, the moment a witness becomes disqualified is the moment of his act — not the court's verdict. This shapes how earlier testimonies he gave are treated.

📅 The Nisan Timeline

R' Yose's principle in action: any testimony given between act and exposure is void.
1️⃣ 1 Nisan: defendant allegedly steals an ox.
↓
2️⃣ 10 Nisan: witnesses come to court and testify he stole on 1 Nisan, and slaughtered/sold on 10 Nisan.
↓
3️⃣ 10–15 Nisan: those same witnesses testify in other cases.
↓
4️⃣ 15 Nisan: they are הוזמו — proven to be perjurers.
↓
5️⃣ Result: כָּל עֵדוּת שֶׁהֵעִידוּ מֵעֲשָׂרָה בְנִיסָן עַד חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בְּנִיסָן לְמַפְרֵיעָהּ הֲרֵי אֵילּוּ פְסוּלוֹת — every testimony in that 5-day window is retroactively disqualified.
The teaching: Once a witness is exposed, the court treats him as having been disqualified from the moment of his perjury — not the moment of his exposure. R' Abba bar Mamal narrows this to cases where they testified together in one group.

🔁 Sequential Witnesses Reactivated

Two scenarios where a previously rejected testimony is accepted because of a second pair.

📥 Scenario A

  • Theft witnesses arrive — court does not accept
  • Slaughter witnesses arrive later
  • Court tells them: we'll accept the theft testimony על ידיכם
  • If slaughter witnesses are zomemim → they pay על יד עידי גניבה

📤 Scenario B (R' Chizkiyah)

  • Slaughter witnesses arrive first — not accepted
  • Theft witnesses arrive later
  • Court tells them: slaughter testimony is now accepted על ידיכם
  • If theft witnesses are zomemim → they pay על יד עידי טביחה

🔗 Common Move

  • A pair's testimony can sit dormant
  • The next pair 'activates' it
  • Liability flows backward through that activation
Why both versions matter: The sugya isn't sure which pair has the activating role. Either ordering shows the same logical structure: testimony only counts once joined to a corroborating pair.

📜 Mishnah 7:4 — Lone Witness on Slaughter

When the second leg of the case rests on weaker evidence, ד׳ וה׳ falls away.
Theft proven bySlaughter/sale proven byPenalty
Two witnessesTwo witnessesד׳ וה׳
Two witnessesOne witnessכפל only
Two witnessesDefendant's own confessionכפל only
Two witnesses (Shabbat / ע״ז / father died / הקדיש)Two witnesses but the act has another issueכפל only
The structural rule: The penalty climbs in proportion to the strength of the proof. Without two valid witnesses to the slaughter/sale, you keep the theft penalty (כפל) but lose the multiplier.

🐄 R' Shimon's Position on Hekdesh

When the stolen animal is consecrated, does it still trigger ד׳ וה׳?
Tanna kamma:
גנב והקדיש ואחר כך טבח
⇒ כפל only
↓
R' Shimon refines:
↓
קדשים שחייב באחריותן
He still bears the responsibility
⇒ ד׳ וה׳
קדשים שאינו חייב באחריותן
Not 'his' anymore
⇒ פטור
↓
R' Shimon links the penalty to the thief's continuing ownership-stake in the animal.
The Yerushalmi's question: Who taught the rule that 'one penalty before court excludes another'? R' Shimon — because his version of the rule for hekdesh tracks ownership-responsibility, the same logic that limits ד׳ וה׳ here.
