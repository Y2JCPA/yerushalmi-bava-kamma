📖 Overview — Who Owns the Goring Ox?

שׁוֹר שֶׁנָּגַח אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה שְׁוָורִין... שְׁמִירַת נְזָקִין כִּשְׁמִירַת קִנְיָין
The sugya: The Mishnah pictures a tam ox that gores several other oxen and is now owed to many victims. R' Yehudah and R' Meir disagree about whether the damaged party becomes a partial owner of the goring ox or only holds it as a pledge. The Yerushalmi runs the dispute through four practical scenarios — dedication, price-rise, rental, and improvement — to show what is at stake. The sugya then shifts to a related partnership Mishnah: when three put unequal amounts into a common purse, how do they share gains and losses?

💬 R' Meir's Two-Sided Attack

R' Yannai sets up R' Meir's dilemma against the goring ox's owner.
RY
R' Yannai: בָּא עָלָיו רִבִּי מֵאִיר מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין — R' Meir comes at him from two sides.
RM
מַה נַפְשָׁךְ: if the ox is yours — give it to me as payment! If it isn't yours — give it to me so I can collect from it!
Q
But didn't R' Eleazar say שְׁמִירַת נְזָקִין כִּשְׁמִירַת קִנְיָין — guarding for damages is like guarding ownership?
RJ
R' Yudan: it was handed over to him for his damages, but it remains כְּמַשְׁכּוֹן בְּיָדוֹ — like a pledge in his hand, not full ownership.
The fault line: Is the goring ox transferred to the נזק as payment (R' Meir: shared ownership) or held only as collateral until value is recovered (R' Yehudah: pledge)?

📐 Four Test Cases — R' Yehudah vs. R' Meir

R' Yochanan: the dispute plays out in four real-life scenarios.
ScenarioR' Yehudah (pledge)R' Meir (joint ownership)
הֶקְדֵּשׁ — dedicating itFirst (original) owner alone may dedicateBoth must dedicate together
הוֹקִיר — went up in valueIncrease belongs to the firstIncrease belongs to the second (the נזק)
עָשָׂה שָׂכָר — rented it outRent goes to the firstRent goes to both
הִשְׁבִּיחַ — improvedImprovement to the firstImprovement to both
Why this matters: R' Yochanan does not abstractly resolve the dispute. He shows us what hangs on it: every benefit and burden of the ox sorts itself differently depending on whether the נזק is a partner or just a pledge-holder.

🤝 R' Yehudah and R' Shimon — Same Word, Different Scope

R' Yochanan's second observation: both Tannaim use 'קנה', but mean different things.

🟢 R' Yehudah

  • קָנָה — he acquired
  • Acquired both the living and the dead
  • Strong form: he is a real partner in the ox itself

🔴 R' Shimon

  • קָנָה — he acquired
  • Acquired only the dead (the carcass of the gored)
  • Weaker form: not a partner in the living goring ox
R' Yochanan's nuance: שְׁנֵיהֶן אָמְרוּ דָבָר אֶחָד — they agree on the word but not its reach. Halakhic vocabulary is precise: the same verb can mean very different things in different mouths.

💼 Three Partners Throw Into a Purse

The sugya turns to a parallel partnership case.
שלשה שהטילו לכיס
Three put different amounts into a shared purse
↓
They gain or lose — how do they split?
↓
מרגלית (precious stone)
R' Bun: split by investment — without your 10 dinarim it could not be bought
Standard goods
Default: pool and split proportionally
R' Eleazar's challenge
Even ordinary goods → split evenly: small inventory turns over fast
↓
R' Eleazar's market reality: אֲנָא הֲפַךְ וּמִתְהַפֵּךְ בְּדִידִי וּמַטִּי בָךְ — I turn mine over and catch up to you
Conceptual link: Just as the goring-ox sugya asks 'how does the נזק's claim translate into ownership?', this Mishnah asks 'how does each partner's investment translate into a share of the profit?' Both turn on what is being divided — capital, labor, or risk.

🌍 The Rome Hypothetical

R' Eleazar pushes the case across the Mediterranean.
1️⃣ Two partners: one with much merchandise, one with little.
↓
2️⃣ Both have stock in the same market — small partner turns over goods quickly and catches up.
↓
3️⃣ But what if one partner's goods are in Rome?
↓
4️⃣ Small partner: עַד דְּאַתְּ סְלִיק לְרוֹמֵי אֲנַא הֲפַךְ וּמִתְהַפֵּךְ בְּדִידִי — by the time you travel to Rome, I've already flipped my stock.
↓
5️⃣ Even with absent inventory, the local partner has earned an equal share through speed of trade.
The principle: Capital is not the only kind of contribution. Time, motion, and the ability to keep money working in the market are themselves a form of investment that the halakhah recognizes.
