📖 Overview — Stopping, Running, and Splitting Wood

כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָמַד נַעֲשֶׂה כְקֶרֶן זָוִית
The sugya: The daf strings together a series of refinements. Rav says the rule of the standing beam-carrier only applies when he מְמַלָּא כָּל רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. Yose HaBavli rules that running is itself a deviation that creates liability — except at twilight on Erev Shabbat, when running is normal. The wood-splitter case asks whether saying 'come in' creates a partnership-courtyard. Then the chapter shifts to two oxen wounding each other and the calculation of הַמּוֹתָר.

💬 The Standing Walker — Like a Street Corner

Rav's qualification of the Mishnah's rule.
Rav
The Mishnah's liability for stopping only applies בִּמְמַלָּא כָּל רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — when his beam blocks the entire road.
Q
But our Mishnah's beam-carrier did not necessarily fill the whole road — so why is he liable for stopping?
A
כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָמַד נַעֲשֶׂה כְקֶרֶן זָוִית — the moment he stopped, he became a 'street corner' — a fixed obstacle the next walker can't anticipate.
Y
Walking and stopping are categorically different. A pedestrian flow allows reaction; a sudden corner does not.
Key concept: קֶרֶן זָוִית is the legal image: a stationary projection into a moving stream. That image converts a pedestrian into a hazard.

🏃 Yose HaBavli — The Running Pedestrian

When ordinary public-domain motion crosses into deviation.
1️⃣ Mishnah: two running, or one running one walking — if they damage each other, both exempt.
↓
2️⃣ Yose HaBavli pushes back: הָיָה רָץ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְהִזִּיק חַיָיב — running is a deviation from the norm.
↓
3️⃣ שֶׁשִּׁינָּה הַמִּנְהָג — once you broke from normal pace, you assumed the risk.
↓
4️⃣ Exception: עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת — twilight on Friday, running to finish before Shabbat is itself the norm.
↓
5️⃣ Aside: Yose HaBavli is identified as Yose ben Yehudah, also known as Yose Katontah — שֶׁהָיָה קְטָן חֲסִידִים, the youngest/last of the pious.
Halakhic move: Liability tracks מִנְהָג. Whatever a society normally does in a space sets the baseline; deviating from it transfers responsibility.

🪓 The Wood-Splitter and the Worker

R' Yose ben Chanina's case opens a layered debate.

👁️ R' Yose ben Chanina

  • Splitting wood in his courtyard
  • Worker enters to claim wages
  • Splinter ricochets, hits worker
  • Ruling: חַיָיב for damages
  • But exempt from גלות if worker dies

📕 R' Chiyya's Beraita

  • Same case → פָּטוּר
  • Resolution: לֹא פְלִיגֵי
  • R' Yose: when he saw him
  • R' Chiyya: when he didn't see

🤝 "Come In" Theory

  • Once he says הִיכָּנֵס
  • Becomes חָצֵר הַשּׁוּתָּפִין
  • Partners are mutually liable for damages
  • R' Yochanan in name of R' Yannai
The deeper move: Inviting someone in is a halakhic act. It transforms a private courtyard into a partnership space, importing the rule that partners owe each other for damages.

🐂 Two Oxen — Paying the Difference (בַּמּוֹתָר)

How the Mishnah computes mutual goring damage.
Two oxen wound each other
What does each owe?
↓
Both תָם (untrained)
Pay בַּמּוֹתָר חֲצִי נֶזֶק
half of the net difference
Both מוּעָד (forewarned)
Pay בַּמּוֹתָר נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם
full damages on the difference
מוּעָד vs. תָם
Mu'ad → full;
Tam → half
↓
R' Akiva: even a tam goring a human pays full damages on the מוֹתָר.
↓
Computation: subtract the smaller liability from the larger; the net is what changes hands.
Why מוֹתָר math: When both parties owe each other, justice requires netting — not gross payment. The Mishnah treats mutual injury as a single accounting problem.
