📖 Overview — Asymmetry Between Person and Ox

יֵשׁ חַיָיב עַל מַעֲשֵׂה שׁוֹרוֹ וּפָטוּר עַל מַעֲשֵׂה עַצְמוֹ. פָּטוּר עַל מַעֲשֵׂה שׁוֹרוֹ וּחַיָיב עַל מַעֲשֵׂה עַצְמוֹ
The sugya: The Mishnah teases out cases where a person and his ox produce opposite liabilities — sometimes the ox triggers payment while the owner himself is exempt, and sometimes the reverse. The unifying principle behind the exemption pattern is קַם לֵיהּ בְּדְרַבָּה מִינֵּיהּ — when the act carries a capital penalty, monetary liability falls away. Ben Pedaya then frames the broader principle: spoilers (מְקַלְקְלִין) on Shabbat are exempt except the מבעיר and the one who wounds. The chapter then turns to the serial gorer.

The pattern: An animal cannot be tried capitally as a moral agent in these cases. So pure damages still apply. A human, however, when the same act carries death or other supreme penalty, escapes the lesser monetary obligation.

🔥 Why Person Is Exempt Where Ox Is Liable

Three different exemption mechanisms working in parallel.

👨‍👩‍👦 Striking Parent

  • וּמַכֵּה אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ מוֹת יוּמָת
  • Capital crime if blood is drawn
  • Money obligation absorbed by death penalty
  • Bull, not subject to that law, just pays

🔥 Burning on Shabbat

  • Hav'arah is one of 39 melakhot
  • Capital (with witnesses & warning)
  • פָּטוּר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּידּוֹן בְּנַפְשׁוֹ
  • Bull has no Shabbat liability → still pays

👁️ Slave's Eye / Tooth

  • Person liable: slave goes free
  • But this is the Torah's special remedy
  • Bull cannot trigger manumission
  • So bull is exempt, owner-by-self is liable
Sharp insight: The Mishnah's mirror-cases are not arbitrary curiosities — they map the precise gap between human moral agency (capital, manumission) and the bull as mere mazik.

💬 Ben Pedaya — Spoilers Are Exempt

Generalizing the Shabbat exemption.
Ben P
כָּל הַמְקַלְקְלִין פְּטוּרִין חוּץ מִן הַמַּבְעִיר וְהָעוֹשֶׂה חַבּוּרָה — destructive Shabbat acts don't generate Shabbat liability, except kindling fire and wounding.
RY
R' Yochanan refines: the מבעיר is liable only עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא צָרִיךְ לָאֶפֶר — when he needs the ash. The wounder is liable only when he needs the blood.
Q
Difficulty: but the Mishnah said the bull who burned the stack is liable! Did it need the ash?
A
שׁוֹרוֹ צָרִיךְ אֶת הָאֶפֶר — yes, his bull needs the ash. The Mishnah's case fits the requirement, so the owner pays for tort even though the burning occurred on Shabbat.
The chain: Shabbat capital liability requires a constructive act — מְלֶאכֶת מַחְשֶׁבֶת. Pure destruction is exempt unless it serves a productive end (ash, blood).

🐂 The Serial Gorer — R' Meir's Rule

How a bull's value gets distributed across multiple victims.
One bull gores 4 or 5 bulls in sequence
↓
יְשַׁלֵּם לָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁבָּהֶן — pay the last victim first.
↓
Leftover after last
Reverts to penultimate
Still leftover
Reverts to the one before that
R' Meir's rule
הָאַחֲרוֹן אַחֲרוֹן נִשְׂכָּר
last victim wins
↓
R' Shimon disputes: with two equal bulls and worthless carcass, each owner takes a מנה — value-based split, not seniority.
Logic of seniority: By the time the bull gored the second victim, the first victim already had a lien on the bull's value. R' Meir reverses the intuition: each new attack creates a fresh lien, and the most recent claim collects from a now-encumbered asset.

Bottom line: The daf maps the seams in liability — between agent and animal, between destruction and creation, between earlier and later claimants on the same damaged asset.
