📖 Overview — Collisions in the Public Domain

שְׁנֵי קַדָּרִין שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין זֶה אַחַר זֶה וְנִתְקַל הָרִאשׁוֹן וְנָפַל וְנִתְקַל הַשֵּׁנִי בָּרִאשׁוֹן הָרִאשׁוֹן חַיָיב בְּנִזְקֵי הַשֵּׁנִי
The sugya: The Mishnah opens a new sub-topic: damage between two people both legitimately walking in רשות הרבים. The Yerushalmi pushes on the timing edges (does the first get a moment to stand?), extends the case to three, four, even a hundred, and then maps a hierarchy of right-of-way rules — loaded vs. empty, ships vs. carts, even bathhouse vs. toilet etiquette.

🏺 Two Potters Walking in File

The base case the Mishnah opens with.

1️⃣ First Stumbles

  • נִתְקַל הָרִאשׁוֹן וְנָפַל
  • Becomes a hazard in the path
  • Effectively a בור the second can't avoid

2️⃣ Second Falls Over Him

  • נִתְקַל הַשֵּׁנִי בָּרִאשׁוֹן
  • His pots break on the first one's body
  • Mishnah: הָרִאשׁוֹן חַיָיב בְּנִזְקֵי הַשֵּׁנִי

🚧 Why Liable?

  • Once down, he should warn or move
  • The Yerushalmi treats him like a stationary obstacle
  • Compare 16a: standing in the road = קֶרֶן זָוִית
Key move: The first potter is no longer just a walker. After he falls, he becomes a hazard — and ownership of the hazard means liability for what trips on it.

💬 R' Yochanan's Question — A Moment to Stand?

How long after the fall does liability attach?
RY
מַהוּ שֶׁיִּתֵּן לָרִאשׁוֹן שָׁהוּת כְּדֵי עֲמִידָה? — Do we give the first potter time to get up before holding him liable?
R' Yose
פְּשִׁיטָא לְרִבִּי יוֹחָנָן לִיתֵּן לָרִאשׁוֹן שָׁהוּת — for R' Yochanan it is obvious we grant him time to stand.
Y
But what about the next one in line — does he get the same grace period? Probably not: from the second onwards, you saw the chain reaction and should know to stop.
Beraita
Three, four, a hundred fall in a chain — first pays second, second pays third. וְאִם מַחֲמַת הָרִאשׁוֹן נָפְלוּ כוּלָּן הוּא מְשַׁלֵּם עַל יְדֵי כוּלָּן.
Important caveat: Liability is for damage to people; for damage to vessels in the chain, all are exempt — the Torah only obligates an animal/person who actively damaged, not a passive victim turned obstacle.

💥 R' Ze'ira — When They Veered (שִׁנּוּ)

The amphora vs. beam case opens an edge question.
1️⃣ Mishnah: one walks with a jug, one with a beam — neither liable, both have רְשׁוּת לְהַלֵּךְ.
↓
2️⃣ R' Ze'ira asks: שִׁנּוּ וְהִזִּיקוּ מַהוּ? — what if one veered out of his lane and damaged?
↓
3️⃣ Compare: five sat on a bench; a sixth sat down and it broke.
↓
4️⃣ The sixth alone pays — he is the one who changed the equilibrium.
↓
5️⃣ But if it broke מֵחֲמַת כּוּלָּן, all are liable together.
Bottom line: Permission to walk does not include permission to deviate. Once you change your trajectory and that change causes damage, the exemption of רְשׁוּת לְהַלֵּךְ evaporates.

Same logic flips when the jug-carrier is in front — the front one bears liability if he stops without warning.
↓
וְכֵן זֶה בָּא בְנֵירוֹ וְזֶה בָּא בְפִשְׁתָּנוֹ — same rules for fire-bearer and flax-bearer.
The principle: Walking is licensed; stopping silently is not. A sudden halt converts a walker into a hazard, and warning shifts the responsibility to the one behind.
