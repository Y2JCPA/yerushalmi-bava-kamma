📖 Overview — What 'The Owner Is Free' Really Means

וּבַעַל הַשּׁוֹר נָקִי. נָקִי מֵחֲצִי כוֹפֶר. דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. אָמַר לוֹ רִבִּי עֲקִיבָה. רִבִּי. וַהֲלֹא מוּקְדָּם הוּא לְבֵית הַסְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה
The sugya: Exodus 21:28 says that when an ox gores a person to death, the ox is stoned and 'the owner of the bull is free' (וּבַעַל הַשּׁוֹר נָקִי). R' Eliezer reads this as freeing him from half the kofer (weregilt). R' Akiva pushes back: the owner is already destined for the stoning place — how can the verse be talking about him? The Yerushalmi tracks both opinions through five distinct moves: a frontal challenge, a narrowing, an internal critique, alternative readings of the exemption, and a closing gezerah shavah about which version of 'the bull shall be stoned' anchors the law.

💬 R' Eliezer vs. R' Akiva — Round One

What does the Torah free the owner from?
RE
וּבַעַל הַשּׁוֹר נָקִי — free from half the kofer. When the bull is a tam that kills a person, the owner is exempt from paying half-weregilt.
RA
רִבִּי, וַהֲלֹא מוּקְדָּם הוּא לְבֵית הַסְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה? — Rabbi, isn't he already destined for the severity of the stoning place? How can the verse even discuss money?
RE
לֹא אָמַרְתִּי אֶלָּא בְּנִתְכַּוֵון לַהֲרוֹג אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה וְהָרַג אֶת הָאָדָם — I only spoke about a case where the bull intended to kill an animal but ended up killing a person. Then there is no death penalty for the owner, and the kofer-exemption is meaningful.
Key move: R' Akiva is not denying the exemption. He is pointing out that for a typical murderous-bull case, the owner faces capital liability anyway — so the verse can't be his concern. R' Eliezer answers by carving out the unintended-killing case where the owner avoids the stoning court.

🔎 The Yerushalmi's Internal Critique

Should R' Akiva's question apply just as much to himself?
R' Akiva attacks R' Eliezer:
The owner is already going to בית הסקילה.
↓
עַד דּוּ מַקְשֵׁי לָהּ עַל דְּרִבִּי לִעֶזֶר יַקְשֶׁינָּהּ עַל דִּידֵיהּ
Before he objects to R' Eliezer, shouldn't he object to himself?
↓
R' Miasha
תִּיפְתָּר בְּשֶׁעָבַר וּשְׁחָטוֹ
Read it about a case where he transgressed and slaughtered the bull — so no stoning court for the owner here either.
Alternative reading
R' Akiva himself uses the same narrowing as R' Eliezer: the case is when the bull intended to kill an animal, or a Gentile, or a stillbirth.
↓
Either way, R' Akiva's exemption — like R' Eliezer's — only operates outside the standard capital case.
Yerushalmi style: The Yerushalmi will not let an Amora score points cheaply. If R' Akiva's question would also undermine his own reading, the Yerushalmi forces him to live with the same answer.

🆚 Two Readings of וּבַעַל הַשּׁוֹר נָקִי

Each Tanna assigns the exemption to a different liability.

🐂 R' Eliezer

  • נָקִי מֵחֲצִי כוֹפֶר — free from half the weregilt
  • Operative case: tam that intended an animal but killed a person
  • Verse אִם כּוֹפֶר יוּשַׁת עָלָיו speaks of the mu'ad — full kofer there
  • So 'נקי' here must address the tam — exempt from half

📜 R' Akiva

  • נָקִי מִדְּמֵי עֶבֶד — free from paying for a slave
  • Worried about the stoning court collision
  • Reads the exemption against the slave-payment rule of Ex. 21:32
  • Forces the case into the same narrow scenarios R' Eliezer used

🤝 Common ground

  • Both agree the verse cannot mean a standard murderous-bull case
  • Both narrow it to where the owner is not capitally liable
  • They differ only on which monetary payment the verse waives
Why the dispute matters: It's not a vague hermeneutic preference. R' Eliezer needs נָקִי for half-kofer because the verse אִם כּוֹפֶר יוּשַׁת already covers the mu'ad's full kofer. R' Akiva reroutes the exemption to the slave-payment because in his system the half-kofer is handled differently.

🧠 The Closing Gezerah Shavah

Which 'the bull shall be stoned' is the source — and which is the recipient?
1️⃣ The Torah twice uses similar phrases: הַשּׁוֹר יִסָּקֵל (in our parsha, on tam) and הַשּׁוֹר יִסָּקֵל (later, on mu'ad).
↓
2️⃣ R' Akiva: just as 'the bull shall be stoned' there speaks of the mu'ad, so 'the bull shall be stoned' here also gets read in the mu'ad's framework — yielding the half-kofer exemption for the tam.
↓
3️⃣ Objection: but elsewhere the Torah writes סָקוֹל יִסָּקֵל הַשּׁוֹר — wouldn't that be the better link? Doubled language is usually weightier.
↓
4️⃣ Resolution: מוּטָּב יִלָּמֵד הַשּׁוֹר יִסָּקֵל מִשּׁוֹר יִסָּקֵל — better that 'the bull shall be stoned' learn from a phrase that matches it exactly.
↓
5️⃣ Principle: a gezerah shavah prefers identical phrasing over a near-identical doubled phrasing.
Bottom line: The daf, which on the surface is a short three-line dispute, actually contains: a frontal Tannaitic clash, a self-objection move (יַקְשֶׁינָּהּ עַל דִּידֵיהּ), two parallel exemption-mappings, and a meta-rule about how to choose between competing gezerot shavot.
